# CCAI Research Seminars - Speaker Guide

Welcome to the research seminar at the **Centre for Credible AI (CCAI)**.
Will you be presenting at one of our seminars? Here is some information that you might find useful.

We run three formats of presentations. Please read the one that applies to you carefully before your session.

---

## Type 1: Invited Expert Talk

**Goal:** Give the audience a broad, accessible view of (your) a research area, the landscape of open problems, key results, and where the field is heading. A secondary goal is to identify potential for future collaboration with CCAI.

**Who:** External researchers invited by the organizers.

**Format:**
- Length: ~45 minutes + 15 minutes Q&A
- Slides are expected; no strict template
- The talk should be self-contained and accessible to an interdisciplinary audience (ML researchers, statisticians, ML engineers)

**What to cover:**
- What is the core problem you work on, and why does it matter?
- What are the main open challenges in the area?
- What has your group contributed, and what are you working on now?
- Where do you see natural points of contact with CCAI's work on credible, explainable, and trustworthy AI?

**Logistics:** Please send your title and a short abstract (3–5 sentences) to the organizers at least one week in advance so we can announce the talk.

---

## Type 2: Internal Deep-Dive (Paper Presentation)

**Goal:** A thorough, critical walkthrough of a selected paper from a peer-reviewed venue, making it accessible and useful for the whole group.

**Who:** CCAI staff and students.

**Paper selection (done before the session):**
1. At least **one week before** your slot, propose **3 candidate papers** in the `#seminar` Slack channel. Each paper must have been published at a peer-reviewed reputable conference (A*) or journal **within the last 2 years**.
2. Include a one-sentence motivation for each candidate.
3. The group votes using emoji reactions; the most-voted paper wins.

**Presentation structure (45 min + 15 min discussion):**

1. **The team behind the paper** *(~2-3 min)*
   - Who are the authors? What institution/group are they from?
   - What is their broader research agenda?

2. **Reception and impact** *(~2-3 min)*
   - Where was the paper published (venue, tier)?
   - Is it popular/cited, where? in what timeframe?
   - Has it sparked follow-up work or been contested?
   - Any reviews on https://openreview.net/?

3. **Main contributions** *(~30 min)*
   - State the problem clearly before diving into the solution.
   - Walk through the key ideas, methods, and results in enough depth that someone unfamiliar with the paper can follow.
   - Use figures from the paper — explain them carefully rather than assuming they are self-evident.
   - Highlight what is genuinely new compared to prior work.

4. **Critical reflection** *(~7 min)*
   - What are the limitations or open questions left by the paper?
   - What would you do differently, or what would be a natural next step?

**Tip:** Assume your audience has not read the paper. Your job is to make them feel like they have, and to have an opinion about it ;-)

---

## Type 3: ,,Dirty'' Reading Group

**Goal:** Share enthusiasm about something interesting you found recently, no slides/polishing required.

**Who:** Anyone in the group.

**What counts:** papers, preprints, blog posts, technical reports, informal write-ups, even a Twitter/X thread that sparked an idea. The work does not need to be peer-reviewed.

**Format:**
- No slides necessary, you can simply pull up the paper or post and walk through it live.
- Length is flexible, typically 30–50 minutes including discussion.
- The tone is conversational. Stop whenever something is interesting, ask questions, go off on tangents.
- The goal is **shared curiosity**, not completeness.

**How to propose:** Drop a link in `#seminar` on Slack with a short note on why you find it exciting. Having people interested in this topic we can run it as a reading club.

---

## General Notes for All Speakers

- Don’t go crazy with the number of slides. If you have more slides than minutes in your presentation, that’s a bad sign. It’s better to show fewer slides, but in a way that ensures we understand exactly what’s going on. **For complex topics, it’s perfectly fine to allow at least two minutes per slide.**
- Avoid having too much text on a slide. Generally, we only have one language processor, so if we have to read something, we won’t be listening
- **Upload your materials** to the seminar repository after the session.
- If possible make a link/QR code to full results.
- If you are presenting **online**, please join a few minutes early to test audio and screen sharing.
- All talks are recorded for internal use unless the speaker opts out, let the organizer know in advance.
- Questions and interruptions during the talk are welcome and encouraged.

# CCAI Research Seminar — Code of Conduct

The seminar is a space built on shared curiosity. We gather because we find the same problems fascinating, not to demonstrate how much we know, but to understand more together. The rest of the document is a reminder of what makes a seminar worth attending.

---

## The spirit of the seminar

Science is hard. Preparing a talk is harder. Someone spent hours reading, thinking, distilling, and standing in front of a room to share something they found interesting. That deserves respect, regardless of whether the paper was groundbreaking, the slides were polished, or the speaker was nervous.

We are not here to perform intelligence at each other. We are here to think together.

---

## As an audience member

**Lead with curiosity, not critique.**
Your first instinct after a result surprises or confuses you should be a question, not a verdict. "I don't understand how X leads to Y, can you walk through that?" is almost always more productive than "I don't think X leads to Y."

**Acknowledge the work before improving it.**
If you see a flaw, a gap, or a better approach - great. Say so. But frame it as a contribution to the conversation, not a correction of the speaker. "This is interesting, have you considered what happens if..." lands differently than "But you didn't account for.."

**Ask questions that open things up, not close them down.**
The best seminar questions are the ones that make the speaker think, not the ones that make the audience think you already knew the answer.

**Let the speaker finish their thought.**
Interruptions can be energising, but only when they are generous. If you are about to jump in, ask yourself: am I helping this idea develop, or am I redirecting attention to myself?

**Silence is not indifference, but engagement is a gift.**
You do not have to ask a question, but if something genuinely interested or puzzled you, sharing that is a contribution to everyone in the room.

---

## What we do not do

- We do not ask questions designed to embarrass.
- We do not use Q&A as a platform for our own views while nominally addressing the speaker.
- We do not dismiss results because they are "just a preprint" or "only a blog post" — every format has value in a field that moves this fast.
- We do not make anyone feel that their area of work is less serious or less interesting than ours.
- We do not treat the seminar as a competition.

---
 
## Laptops
 
Please keep your laptop closed during the seminar. The sound of typing is distracting, not just for the speaker, but for everyone around you. If you have something genuinely urgent to deal with, connect remotely from the room next door rather than working in the back of the room.

---

## A note on disagreement

Disagreement is healthy and welcome. The seminar should produce friction, the productive kind, where two people who see something differently both leave with a sharper view. What we ask is that disagreement is directed at ideas, delivered with the assumption that the other person is smart and well-intentioned, and expressed in a way that invites a response rather than ending a conversation.

If you think something is wrong, say so clearly and explain why. Then listen.

---

## For the speaker

You are not expected to know everything. "I don't know" and "that's a great question I haven't thought about" are legitimate and respected answers. The seminar is not an exam.

---

## The one-sentence version

Come fascinated, leave having learned something, and make sure the person who presented feels it was worth the effort.
